Current research interests

The intersection of geometry, topology, representation, and model behavior.

Modern AI models are treated as an engineering-first problem: more data, more layers, more complexity in mechanisms, little understanding of what is going on under the hood. My work aims to understand the structure of learned representations, specifically how they can be measured, calibrated, and connected to decision-relevant model outcomes. To support this endeavor, I utilize mathematical tools (geometry, topology, optimal transport) to analyze the high-dimensional spaces generated through AI model training. This research is distinct from approaches such as 'mechanistic interpretability', instead focusing on mathematically grounded notions to measure robustness at scale. This page collects my current research directions, supporting repositories, publications, and earlier projects.

Current directions

Benchmarked datasets, optimization mechanisms, and model architectures are the main drivers in current AI paradigms. These all contribute to a wealth of internal representations that are mainly studied through post-hoc analyses that become academic research papers but are not operationalized as interventions. My current research focus is in identifying opportunities where models drift toward 'collapsed states', where the internal representations lose richness in cluster structure, become highly anisotropic, and destroy topological features that provide degrade important signals for downstream performance. This requires significant statistical calibration to serve as an intervention mechanism in a low-latency way that balances maintenance of these important structures with behavior-based outcomes.

Decision-relevant representation measurement

Developing calibrated geometric and topological measurements for learned representations, with an emphasis on distinguishing meaningful structure from finite-sample artifacts.

Fine-tuning trajectory monitoring

Tracking whether collapse-like signals emerge, intensify, or disappear during fine-tuning and safety-tuning, and relating those signals to model behavior.

Publications

Selected publications (and pre-prints) listed in reverse chronological order. These publications represent work that was done for geometric, topological, and knowledge integration algorithms over large point clouds, such as LLM and knowledge graph embedding spaces.

Monitoring Neural Training with Topology: A Footprint-Predictable Collapse Index

Alex Kalinowski. Preprint, 2026.

Persistent homology computations require generating a filtration for each point cloud in a sequence; this is inefficient when the cloud exhibits minor shifts and ignores information from a prior step in the sequence. A Modular Morse Homology Maintenance engine is used for online evolutions, coupled with a footprint-bound index that predicts collapse earlier than alternative methods.

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26984
2026

Calibrated Persistent Homology Tests for High-dimensional Collapse Detection

Alex Kalinowski. Computational Geometry Week, year.

Utilizing persistent homology metrics to detect point cloud collapse requires statistical calibration; we utilize this calibration to develop a mechanism map for where and when certain metrics shine.

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26068
